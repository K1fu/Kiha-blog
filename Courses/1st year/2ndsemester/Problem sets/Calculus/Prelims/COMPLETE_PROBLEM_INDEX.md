### LESSON 2.1: Introduction to Limits

**Problem M2-1-A1** [Difficulty: Easy | Time: 3 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to 2} (3x + 1)$ by direct substitution.

**Answer:** 7

**Solution:**
Since $f(x) = 3x + 1$ is a polynomial, it's continuous everywhere.
$$\lim_{x \to 2} (3x + 1) = 3(2) + 1 = 7$$

---

**Problem M2-1-A2** [Difficulty: Easy | Time: 3 min]

Use the graph approach: If $f(x) = |x|$, find $\lim_{x \to 0} f(x)$.

**Answer:** 0

**Solution:**
Step 1: Approach from the left
- As $x \to 0^-$, $f(x) = -x \to 0$

Step 2: Approach from the right
- As $x \to 0^+$, $f(x) = x \to 0$

Step 3: Since both one-sided limits equal 0
$$\lim_{x \to 0} |x| = 0$$

---

**Problem M2-1-B1** [Difficulty: Medium | Time: 5 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to 3} \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3}$.

**Answer:** 6

**Solution:**
Step 1: Direct substitution gives $\frac{0}{0}$ (indeterminate)

Step 2: Factor numerator
$$\frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3}$$

Step 3: Cancel common factor (valid since $x \neq 3$ in limit)
$$= x + 3$$

Step 4: Evaluate limit
$$\lim_{x \to 3} (x + 3) = 6$$

---

**Problem M2-1-B2** [Difficulty: Medium | Time: 6 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(2+h)^2 - 4}{h}$.

**Answer:** 4

**Solution:**
Step 1: Expand numerator
$$(2+h)^2 - 4 = 4 + 4h + h^2 - 4 = 4h + h^2$$

Step 2: Factor
$$\frac{4h + h^2}{h} = \frac{h(4 + h)}{h}$$

Step 3: Cancel (valid for $h \neq 0$)
$$= 4 + h$$

Step 4: Evaluate limit
$$\lim_{h \to 0} (4 + h) = 4$$

---

**Problem M2-1-C1** [Difficulty: Hard | Time: 8 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{1+x} - 1}{x}$.

**Answer:** $\frac{1}{2}$

**Solution:**
Step 1: Multiply by conjugate
$$\frac{\sqrt{1+x} - 1}{x} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{1+x} + 1}{\sqrt{1+x} + 1}$$

Step 2: Simplify numerator
$$= \frac{(1+x) - 1}{x(\sqrt{1+x} + 1)} = \frac{x}{x(\sqrt{1+x} + 1)}$$

Step 3: Cancel x
$$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x} + 1}$$

Step 4: Evaluate limit
$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x} + 1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1} + 1} = \frac{1}{2}$$

### LESSON 2.2: Epsilon-Delta Definition

**Problem M2-2-A1** [Difficulty: Easy | Time: 5 min]

Given $\epsilon = 0.1$, find a $\delta > 0$ such that if $|x - 2| < \delta$, then $|3x - 6| < 0.1$.

**Answer:** $\delta = \frac{1}{30}$ or any smaller positive value

**Solution:**
Step 1: Analyze inequality
$$|3x - 6| < 0.1$$
$$|3(x - 2)| < 0.1$$
$$3|x - 2| < 0.1$$

Step 2: Solve for $|x - 2|$
$$|x - 2| < \frac{0.1}{3} = \frac{1}{30}$$

Step 3: Choose $\delta$
$$\delta = \frac{1}{30}$$

**Problem M2-2-D1** [Difficulty: Proof | Time: 15 min]

Prove that $\lim_{x \to 3} (2x - 1) = 5$ using the epsilon-delta definition.

**Answer:** Proof below

**Solution:**

**Given:** $\epsilon > 0$
**To Find:** $\delta > 0$ such that $|x - 3| < \delta \implies |(2x-1) - 5| < \epsilon$

**Scratch Work:**
$$|(2x-1) - 5| < \epsilon$$
$$|2x - 6| < \epsilon$$
$$2|x - 3| < \epsilon$$
$$|x - 3| < \frac{\epsilon}{2}$$

**Formal Proof:**

Let $\epsilon > 0$ be given.

Choose $\delta = \frac{\epsilon}{2}$. Note that $\delta > 0$ since $\epsilon > 0$.

Assume $|x - 3| < \delta$.

Then:
$$|x - 3| < \frac{\epsilon}{2}$$
$$2|x - 3| < \epsilon$$
$$|2x - 6| < \epsilon$$
$$|2x - 1 - 5| < \epsilon$$
$$|(2x - 1) - 5| < \epsilon$$

Therefore, by the definition of limit, $\lim_{x \to 3} (2x - 1) = 5$. ∎

**Problem M2-2-D2** [Difficulty: Proof | Time: 18 min]

Prove that $\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = 4$ using epsilon-delta.

**Answer:** Proof below

**Solution:**

**Preliminary:** For $x \neq 2$, $\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x + 2$

**Scratch Work:**
$$\left|\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} - 4\right| < \epsilon$$
$$|x + 2 - 4| < \epsilon$$
$$|x - 2| < \epsilon$$

**Formal Proof:**

Let $\epsilon > 0$ be given.

Choose $\delta = \epsilon$. Note that $\delta > 0$.

Assume $0 < |x - 2| < \delta$.

Since $x \neq 2$, we can simplify:
$$\left|\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} - 4\right| = |x + 2 - 4| = |x - 2|$$

But $|x - 2| < \delta = \epsilon$.

Therefore, $\left|\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} - 4\right| < \epsilon$.

By the definition of limit, $\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2-4}{x-2} = 4$. ∎

### LESSON 2.6: Infinite Limits

**Problem M2-6-A1** [Difficulty: Easy | Time: 3 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x}$.

**Answer:** $+\infty$

**Solution:**
As $x \to 0^+$ (approaching 0 from the right):
- x is small and positive
- $\frac{1}{x}$ is large and positive
- As x gets smaller, $\frac{1}{x}$ grows without bound

Therefore, $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x} = +\infty$

---

**Problem M2-6-A2** [Difficulty: Easy | Time: 3 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to 0^-} \frac{1}{x^3}$.

**Answer:** $-\infty$

**Solution:**
As $x \to 0^-$:
- x is small and negative
- $x^3$ is also small and negative (odd power preserves sign)
- $\frac{1}{x^3}$ is negative and large in magnitude

Since the exponent is odd, the limit is $-\infty$.

---

**Problem M2-6-B1** [Difficulty: Medium | Time: 5 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to 2^+} \frac{3x - 1}{x - 2}$.

**Answer:** $+\infty$

**Solution:**
Step 1: Analyze numerator as $x \to 2^+$
$$3x - 1 \to 3(2) - 1 = 5 > 0$$

Step 2: Analyze denominator as $x \to 2^+$
- $x - 2 \to 0^+$ (small positive value)

Step 3: Determine sign and magnitude
$$\frac{\text{positive constant}}{\text{small positive}} \to +\infty$$

Therefore, $\lim_{x \to 2^+} \frac{3x - 1}{x - 2} = +\infty$

---

**Problem M2-6-C1** [Difficulty: Hard | Time: 7 min]

Find all vertical asymptotes of $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 9}{x^2 - 5x + 6}$.

**Answer:** $x = 2$ only (removable discontinuity at $x = 3$)

**Solution:**
Step 1: Factor numerator and denominator
$$f(x) = \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{(x-2)(x-3)}$$

Step 2: Simplify (for $x \neq 3$)
$$f(x) = \frac{x+3}{x-2}$$

Step 3: Identify vertical asymptotes
- At $x = 2$: denominator is 0, numerator is 5
  - $\lim_{x \to 2^+} f(x) = +\infty$
  - $\lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x) = -\infty$
  - **Vertical asymptote at x = 2**

- At $x = 3$: both numerator and denominator were 0
  - After cancellation, function is defined
  - **Removable discontinuity, NOT a vertical asymptote**

### LESSON 2.7: Limits at Infinity

**Problem M2-7-A1** [Difficulty: Easy | Time: 3 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x}$.

**Answer:** 0

**Solution:**
As $x \to \infty$, the denominator grows without bound while the numerator remains constant.
Therefore, $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$.

---

**Problem M2-7-A2** [Difficulty: Easy | Time: 3 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to \infty} (2x + 5)$.

**Answer:** $+\infty$

**Solution:**
As $x \to \infty$, both terms grow without bound (positive slope).
Therefore, $\lim_{x \to \infty} (2x + 5) = +\infty$.

---

**Problem M2-7-B1** [Difficulty: Medium | Time: 5 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x + 2}{2x - 1}$.

**Answer:** $\frac{3}{2}$

**Solution:**
Step 1: Divide numerator and denominator by highest power of x
$$\frac{3x + 2}{2x - 1} = \frac{\frac{3x + 2}{x}}{\frac{2x - 1}{x}} = \frac{3 + \frac{2}{x}}{2 - \frac{1}{x}}$$

Step 2: Evaluate limit
$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3 + \frac{2}{x}}{2 - \frac{1}{x}} = \frac{3 + 0}{2 - 0} = \frac{3}{2}$$

Alternative (degree comparison):
- Numerator degree = 1, Denominator degree = 1
- When degrees are equal, limit = ratio of leading coefficients = $\frac{3}{2}$

**Problem M2-7-C1** [Difficulty: Hard | Time: 7 min]

Evaluate $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2x^2 + 3x - 5}{5x^3 - x + 2}$.

**Answer:** 0

**Solution:**
Method 1 - Divide by highest power:
$$\frac{2x^2 + 3x - 5}{5x^3 - x + 2} = \frac{\frac{2}{x} + \frac{3}{x^2} - \frac{5}{x^3}}{5 - \frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{2}{x^3}}$$

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{2}{x} + \frac{3}{x^2} - \frac{5}{x^3}}{5 - \frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{2}{x^3}} = \frac{0 + 0 - 0}{5 - 0 + 0} = 0$$

Method 2 - Degree comparison:
- Numerator degree (2) < Denominator degree (3)
- When numerator degree < denominator degree, limit = 0

### LESSON 3.1: Continuity at a Point

**Problem M3-1-A1** [Difficulty: Easy | Time: 4 min]

Determine if $f(x) = x^2 + 2x + 1$ is continuous at $x = 2$.

**Answer:** Yes, f is continuous at x = 2.

**Solution:**
Step 1: Check if $f(2)$ exists
$$f(2) = 4 + 4 + 1 = 9$$ ✓

Step 2: Check if $\lim_{x \to 2} f(x)$ exists
$$\lim_{x \to 2} (x^2 + 2x + 1) = 9$$ ✓

Step 3: Check if $\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = f(2)$
$$9 = 9$$ ✓

All three conditions satisfied; f is continuous at x = 2.

**Problem M3-1-B1** [Difficulty: Medium | Time: 6 min]

Determine if the following function is continuous at $x = 1$:
$$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 & \text{if } x < 1 \\ 3 & \text{if } x = 1 \\ 2x & \text{if } x > 1 \end{cases}$$

**Answer:** No, f is not continuous at x = 1.

**Solution:**
Step 1: Check if $f(1)$ exists
$$f(1) = 3$$ ✓

Step 2: Check if $\lim_{x \to 1} f(x)$ exists
- Left-hand limit: $\lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 1^-} x^2 = 1$
- Right-hand limit: $\lim_{x \to 1^+} f(x) = \lim_{x \to 1^+} 2x = 2$
- Since $1 \neq 2$, limit does NOT exist ✗

Since the limit doesn't exist, f is not continuous at x = 1.

**Problem M3-1-C1** [Difficulty: Hard | Time: 8 min]

For what value of k is the function continuous at x = 2?
$$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 - 1 & \text{if } x < 2 \\ k & \text{if } x = 2 \\ 3x - 3 & \text{if } x > 2 \end{cases}$$

**Answer:** $k = 3$

**Solution:**
For continuity at x = 2, we need:
$$\lim_{x \to 2^-} f(x) = f(2) = \lim_{x \to 2^+} f(x)$$

Step 1: Find left-hand limit
$$\lim_{x \to 2^-} (x^2 - 1) = 4 - 1 = 3$$

Step 2: Find right-hand limit
$$\lim_{x \to 2^+} (3x - 3) = 6 - 3 = 3$$

Step 3: For continuity
$$f(2) = k = 3$$

Therefore, $k = 3$.

### LESSON 3.3: Types of Discontinuity

**Problem M3-3-A1** [Difficulty: Easy | Time: 5 min]

Classify the discontinuity of $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}$ at $x = 2$.

**Answer:** Removable discontinuity

**Solution:**
Step 1: Check if f(2) exists
$$f(2) = \frac{0}{0}$$ — undefined

Step 2: Check if limit exists
$$\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = \lim_{x \to 2} \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = \lim_{x \to 2} (x+2) = 4$$

Step 3: Classify
- f(2) is undefined
- But $\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 4$ exists
- This is a **removable discontinuity** (can be "fixed" by defining $f(2) = 4$)

---

**Problem M3-3-B1** [Difficulty: Medium | Time: 6 min]

Classify the discontinuity of the following at $x = 3$:
$$f(x) = \begin{cases} x + 2 & \text{if } x < 3 \\ 7 & \text{if } x = 3 \\ 2x - 1 & \text{if } x > 3 \end{cases}$$

**Answer:** Essential discontinuity (jump discontinuity)

**Solution:**
Step 1: Check limits
$$\lim_{x \to 3^-} f(x) = 5$$
$$\lim_{x \to 3^+} f(x) = 5$$

Step 2: Check function value
$$f(3) = 7$$

Step 3: Classify
- Limit exists: $\lim_{x \to 3} f(x) = 5$
- But $f(3) = 7 \neq 5$
- This is actually a **removable discontinuity** (would be continuous if $f(3) = 5$)

Wait, let me recalculate the right limit:
$$\lim_{x \to 3^+} (2x-1) = 6 - 1 = 5$$ ✓

So both one-sided limits are 5, but $f(3) = 7$.
This is indeed **removable discontinuity**.

---

**Problem M3-3-E1** [Difficulty: Trap | Time: 6 min]

True or False: If a function has a removable discontinuity at x = a, then the function must be undefined at x = a.

**Answer:** False

**Solution:**
A removable discontinuity occurs when:
1. $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ exists, AND
2. Either $f(a)$ is undefined OR $f(a) \neq L$

Counterexample:
$$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 & \text{if } x \neq 2 \\ 100 & \text{if } x = 2 \end{cases}$$

At $x = 2$:
- $f(2) = 100$ (defined!)
- $\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 4$
- Since $f(2) \neq \lim_{x \to 2} f(x)$, this is removable discontinuity
- But f(2) IS defined

Therefore, the statement is FALSE.
