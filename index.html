Introduction to Computing - Prelims

The Evolution and Modern Landscape of Computing

This briefing document provides an overview of the history of computing, from its foundational roots in early civilizations to the cutting-edge technologies shaping the modern digital world. It highlights key individuals, innovations, and the profound impact of computing across various industries.

I. The History of Computing: Foundations and Key Figures

The concept of computing, defined as "any activity that utilizes computers to manage, process, and communicate critical information," has a rich history spanning millennia. Early civilizations laid the groundwork for numerical systems and calculation, which evolved into sophisticated mechanical devices and theoretical frameworks before the advent of electronic computers.

A. Early Civilizations and Foundational Concepts:

Babylonians (c. 2000-500 BC)

This civilization thrived in Mesopotamia (Iraq). They utilized "clay cuneiform tables with mathematical texts" for "basic arithmetic operations, cubes, and square roots." Their numerical system was unique, using only two symbols (1 and 10) combined to form all other numbers. They also employed "counting boards," similar to an abacus, for calculations.

Egyptians

They solved practical problems like "measuring time, measuring floods, calculating the land area, bookkeeping, and calculating taxes." They used a base-10 number system with distinct symbols for various magnitudes. The "Rhind Papyrus" served as an instructional text for arithmetic and geometric problems, aiding students in the Pharaoh’s building program.

Greeks (c. 500-600 BC)

They contributed significantly to geometry through figures like Pythagoras and Thales. The Pythagoreans believed "that number was the essence of all things." Thales' theorem defined conditions for a right angle within a circle.

Romans

Influenced by Greek culture, their "Roman number system was one of their most considerable contributions to computing," using letters to symbolize numbers. However, their numerals were "challenging to use, so an abacus was often used for calculation."

Islamic Influence

Islamic mathematicians developed a "comprehensive theory... which used rational and irrational numbers as algebraic objects and moved away from the Greek concept of mathematics as just being about Geometry." They applied algebra to arithmetic and geometry, and studied curves using equations.

B. Notable Figures and Mechanical Calculation:

Wilhelm Gottfried Leibniz

He "developed the binary number system used in digital computers." He designed "The Step Reckoner," the "first calculator to run the basic arithmetic operations" including addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and root extraction.

Charles Babbage

Considered one of the "grandfathers of the computing field," he designed the "Difference Engine" to "precisely compute polynomial functions and logarithmic & trigonometric functions." Ada Lovelace, who worked with him, is widely considered the "first computer programmer."

George Boole

Also a "grandfather of the computing field," Boole developed "Boolean algebra, which is the foundation for modern computing." His work was crucial for "switching theory and digital circuit design."

Grace Brewster Murray Hopper

A computer pioneer and naval officer, she is best known for her "trailblazing involvement in developing computer languages such as COBOL (Common Business-oriented Language)." By the 1970s, COBOL was the "most extensively used computer language."

Katherine Johnson

As a "human computer" for NASA, her geometric calculations helped "figure out the paths for spacecraft to orbit Earth and land on the moon," enabling critical missions.

Gladys Mae West

Her "most outstanding contribution was creating a detailed geodetic model of the Earth, which became the basis for the Global Positioning System (GPS) development."

II. The Modern Computing Landscape

The "rapid increase of mobile devices and the ability to connect things" has ushered in a "post-PC era" characterized by interconnected technologies that deliver on-demand services and process vast amounts of data.

A. Core Modern Computing Technologies:

Cloud Computing

It is the "application of hardware and software to deliver readily available, on-demand, and measured computing services over the Internet." Examples include Google Drive, iCloud, Google Translate, and streaming services.

Virtualization

This technology "simulates hardware functions by creating multiple virtual machines on a physical server." Its essence is to "detach software from hardware by converting ‘physical’ devices into ‘logical’ folders or files."

Internet of Things (IoT)

A "network of an extensive range of physical objectives that automatically share data over the Internet." It facilitates "faster information exchange and communication between ‘things’."

Big Data

Characterized by "massive and complex datasets" with "high volume, variety of data types, and velocity." It is used in banking, healthcare, and education to track and analyze large amounts of information.

Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Allows "machines to learn from experience, adjust to new inputs, and do human-like activities." Examples include facial recognition, voice recognition, chatbots, and virtual assistants.

B. Emerging Computing Paradigms:

Fog Computing

An "expansion of the idea of cloud computing" where data is stored in "edge devices" closer to end-users. It enables local data analytics and real-time data distribution.

Edge Computing

A "decentralized and distributed cloud computing" where the computing infrastructure is "close to data sources." Raw data is "analyzed locally instead of transferred to the cloud."
